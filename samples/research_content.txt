Title: NPvert: Structure-First RAG for LaTeX Document Synthesis

Authors: Nishant Dixit, Pallav Rai

Abstract: Despite their impressive fluency, large language models (LLMs) frequently exhibit an execution illusion: they generate outputs that appear correct at the surface level but fail under formal execution due to syntactic errors, misplaced content, or violations of structural constraints. This limitation is particularly acute in structured document synthesis, where compiler-level correctness is essential. In this work, we introduce NPvert, a Structure-First Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) framework that reconceptualizes document generation as a constrained symbolic placement problem rather than an unconstrained text generation task. NPvert integrates an Abstract Syntax Tree (AST)-based LaTeX parser with a persistent Structure Graph that explicitly encodes document hierarchy, permissible content types, and structural dependencies. Retrieved content is semantically anchored to this graph, enabling the system to identify valid and contextually appropriate insertion points within predefined templates. By separating semantic reasoning from syntactic realization, NPvert guarantees compilable LaTeX and consistently produces high-fidelity PDF outputs. This work demonstrates a principled pathway for combining neural retrieval and reasoning with compiler-enforced determinism, advancing reliable automation for scientific and technical document synthesis.

Introduction: Large Language Models (LLMs) have demonstrated remarkable capabilities in natural language generation, enabling fluent text synthesis across a wide range of applications. However, this fluency often conceals a fundamental limitation when LLMs are applied to tasks governed by strict formal or executable constraints. In the domain of LaTeX document generation, this limitation manifests as outputs that appear syntactically plausible to a human reader but fail to compile due to subtle structural violations, misplaced commands, or invalid nesting of environments. Prior research has characterized this phenomenon as the execution illusion—the discrepancy between linguistic plausibility and executable validity. This challenge is not merely cosmetic. LaTeX is a declarative, structure-sensitive language whose correctness is enforced by a compiler rather than by visual inspection. Minor structural errors, such as inserting content outside an allowed environment or violating section-level constraints, can render an entire document uncompilable. Consequently, naive applications of LLMs to LaTeX synthesis often require extensive manual correction, limiting their usefulness in automation-intensive workflows such as scientific publishing, technical documentation, and large-scale report generation. Existing approaches to structured document generation largely fall into two categories. Rule-based systems and format conversion tools offer speed and determinism but lack semantic understanding, while end-to-end neural generation methods provide flexibility and contextual awareness without guarantees of syntactic validity. Neither paradigm adequately satisfies the dual requirement of semantic reasoning and compiler-level correctness. We argue that this failure stems from a categorical mismatch: LaTeX document synthesis has been treated as a text generation problem rather than as a structured placement problem. We propose NPvert, a structure-first framework in which LLMs are not tasked with producing executable LaTeX directly. Instead, they act as semantic controllers, supporting content classification, section alignment, and contextual relevance, while syntactic realization is delegated to a verified structural representation.

Methodology: NPvert is guided by a single design principle: semantic reasoning and syntactic realization must be explicitly decoupled. Rather than treating document synthesis as monolithic text generation, NPvert formulates it as a structured placement problem, where content is first aligned to valid structural locations and only then rendered into executable LaTeX. The end-to-end pipeline follows the transformation: LaTeX Template + Incoming Content -> Graph-Aware Placement -> Verified PDF Output, ensuring compiler-level correctness at every stage. NPvert performs structure-aware document deconstruction using an AST-based LaTeX parser. The input template is parsed into a hierarchical representation capturing sections, subsections, environments (e.g., figure, table, equation), and command scopes. Content is segmented along semantically and syntactically meaningful boundaries such as section delimiters, environment blocks, and macro definitions. Each resulting unit corresponds to a valid LaTeX fragment and preserves its contextual constraints, eliminating cross-boundary corruption common in token- or paragraph-based chunking strategies. From the parsed AST, NPvert constructs a Structure Graph, formalized as a Directed Acyclic Graph (DAG). Each node represents a structural element of the document. Each node encodes: Structural Constraints (permissible content types and environments), Contextual Dependencies (positional and hierarchical relationships), and Semantic Anchors (compact summaries describing the intended semantic role). This representation enables deterministic validation of all insertion decisions.

Related Work: Several recent works have explored constrained generation and structured document synthesis. Wang et al. (2026) proposed pruning minimal reasoning graphs for efficient RAG. Banerjee et al. (2025) introduced CRANE for constrained reasoning-augmented generation with grammar-constrained decoding. Knollmeyer et al. (2025) developed Document GraphRAG for knowledge-graph-enhanced retrieval in manufacturing. Lyn and Graham (2025) exposed the last-mile execution gap in LLM agents for scientific formatting through TransLaTeX. Other relevant works include RAG-LLM for regulatory documents, RAG-Anything for multimodal LaTeX processing, and various approaches to constrained decoding and symbolic reuse in agentic workflows.

Experiments: We evaluated NPvert on SafeFormatBench, a benchmark of 100 executable LaTeX projects spanning diverse scientific domains. Metrics include compilation rate, placeholder integrity, and formatting compliance. NPvert achieves a 95-96% compilation rate, significantly outperforming free-form LLM generation. The system was tested across multiple domains including computer science, physics, mathematics, and biology. Each test involved providing a template with marked slots and raw content to be inserted. The primary metric was successful compilation without manual intervention. Secondary metrics measured structural fidelity (whether content appeared in correct sections), formatting consistency (adherence to template styles), and semantic coherence (logical flow of inserted content).

Results: NPvert achieved a 95-96% first-attempt compilation rate on SafeFormatBench, compared to 34-42% for direct LLM generation. The structure graph validation caught 89% of potential structural violations before compilation. Average processing time was 4.2 seconds per document. Semantic anchoring accuracy was 91% when evaluated against human-annotated ground truth. The system successfully handled complex nested environments including figures with subfigures, multi-column tables, and algorithm environments. Error analysis revealed that most compilation failures were due to edge cases in math environment handling and custom macro definitions not present in the parser's vocabulary.

Discussion: The results demonstrate that decoupling semantic reasoning from syntactic realization is a viable and effective strategy for structured document generation. By treating LLMs as semantic controllers rather than direct format generators, we eliminate the execution illusion while preserving the models' strengths in content understanding and contextual alignment. The Structure Graph provides an interpretable intermediate representation that enables deterministic validation, a critical capability for production document workflows. Future work should extend the parser to handle custom macro definitions more robustly and explore incremental graph updates for interactive editing scenarios. The framework could also be extended to other structured formats such as HTML with schema validation, XML with DTD enforcement, and Markdown with flavor-specific constraints.

Conclusion: NPvert reframes LaTeX document synthesis as a structure-aware semantic placement problem. By combining graph-enforced correctness with retrieval-assisted reasoning, the framework eliminates the execution illusion and enables reliable, automation-ready scientific document generation. The principled separation of concerns allows each component to excel at its designated task: LLMs handle semantic understanding and content alignment, while the structural layer ensures compiler-level correctness. This architecture provides a template for reliable automation in any domain where formal constraints govern output validity.
